<ArticleSection label="The problem">
<ArticleSubsection>

## The front door was broken

The bar had one job. To be an entry point to get your domain(s) managed by Spaceship. That could be done in two ways: by registering available domains, or transferring ones you already own managed by an external registrar. The numbers were short and that meant the design of the experience was broken.

The input was confusing users on how to achieve each. The idle state inferred searching and transferring could be made in the same place. The placeholder and the buttons available suggested it. But what was really the primary action? What would happen when you hit enter?

It got even more confusing when the input got into focus. A dropdown appeared with two options: Search or Transfer and Bulk transfer. A couple of problems with this: Bulk transfer was in its own place isolated from the transfer mode, and they looked like links, not mode selectors.

<ArticleSection>
<ArticleSubsection>

## What research actually said

First round of usability testing ran on the live product, in the browser. The signal was clean: six issues ranked by severity, the number one problem affecting every single participant.

<figure class="post-figure article-finding article-card">
  <div class="article-finding__nameplate">Research finding #1</div>
  <p class="article-finding__text">Search or transfer a domain placeholder creates confusion—users don't understand the difference between search and transfer, especially with the dropdown open.</p>
</figure>

The research told us what we suspected but needed proof for: registration and transfer are completely different intentions.

Registration is speculative: "Is this available?"

Transfer is intentional: "I own this, move it here."

Cramming both behind the same input meant neither worked properly. The dropdown was doing double duty as both a mode switcher and an explanation of what those modes meant—and failing at both.

Prototypes were done, now just needed to prepare the Design Critique session. The idea was straight forward: prepare a Figma file in advance with the 3 directions, invite 4 designers, and give them the prototypes one day in advance to give them time to sit with them and take notes. Worth flagging—the prototypes used handshake domains as examples. A small way to show the bar could scale to other modes.

<ArticleSection label="User testing">
<ArticleSubsection>

## Testing the prototype

Research confirmed it. Registration and Transfer are 2 different intentions. They shouldn't share a mode. We needed a clear ways to toggle between modes, and the prototype was almost fully successful on that. Almost...

<figure class="post-figure">
  <div class="post-figure__placeholder" role="img" aria-label="The Figma prototype tested — David's direction with toggle inside the bar"></div>
  <figcaption class="post-figure__caption">The Figma prototype we tested—David's direction, toggle inside the bar.</figcaption>
</figure>

Testing with Figma prototypes is not ideal. They're far away from the browser experience. Especially when you have to input text.

Still, the sessions caught real things. The search/transfer confusion from Round 1 didn't resurface once. But 3 of 4 participants looked for transfer in the navigation menu—not the search bar. One put it plainly: "It doesn't seem normal to use a search input for a transfer." The component worked. It just didn't announce itself.

From the sessions I also picked up one interesting thing: participants weren't focusing on the component as naturally as they should—that stayed with me as we moved forward.

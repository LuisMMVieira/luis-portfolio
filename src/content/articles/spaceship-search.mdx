## The front door was broken

Spaceship's homepage search handled both domain registration and domain transfer. A small dropdown labelled "I want to…" switched between the two—silently, with no change to the input itself. The bar looked identical in both modes. The placeholder said "Search or transfer a domain" regardless.

The search bar needed to handle three things: registration, single transfer, and bulk transfer. But in any given mode it wasn't clear what you could actually do with it. And it got worse when the bar was in focus—a dropdown appeared with two options that looked like links, not mode selectors: "Search or Transfer" and "Bulk transfer." What happens when you hit enter? Nobody knew.

  <figcaption class="post-figure__caption">The original search widget. Modes hidden behind a dropdown. Transfer was one accidental click away from registration.</figcaption>

The problem wasn't cosmetic. Registration and transfer are completely different workflows—different inputs, different expectations, different mental models. Registration is speculative: "Is this available?" Transfer is intentional: "I own this, move it here." Cramming both behind the same input meant neither worked properly.

Users who wanted to transfer were accidentally initiating a search, and vice-versa. Users who wanted to transfer couldn't find how. The dropdown was doing double duty as both a mode switcher and an explanation of what those modes meant—and failing at both.

## The room, not just the work

Four teams in the room from day one—Product Platform, Design System, Brand, and Research. A shared roadmap with milestones, research bookending the design work. Not research added at the end to validate decisions already made.

When everyone sees the same timeline, owns their part, and knows where research fits in—the conversations become about the work, not about coordination. That alignment was deliberate. I set it up before any design work started.

<figure class="post-figure article-finding--text-width">
  <img src="/assets/articles/spaceship-search/images/roadmap-gantt.png" alt="Gantt-style roadmap showing all four team swim lanes across November 2023 to January 2024" />
  <figcaption class="post-figure__caption">The roadmap I built and got approved before any design work started. Six weeks, a holiday in the middle, and a hard deadline.</figcaption>

The roadmap was mine. I built it, got it approved, and used it to keep everyone honest for two months. It also defined the design critique structure—create two or three scenarios, make them interactive, gather individual notes before any group discussion, evaluate each prototype, vote preferred and justify why.

I led the process and the roadmap while also being one of the designers. That dual role was intentional—when you're in the Figma file yourself, you have a different kind of authority in the conversations. You're not managing opinions. You're synthesizing while making.

Each proposal attacked the same problem differently. One separated the modes visually but kept them on the same surface. Another introduced progressive disclosure—reveal the mode only after engagement. The third broke them apart entirely into distinct flows.

### The design critique

The design critique was structured: async analysis first, then a live discussion. No presenting, no defending—just talking about the work. What came out of that room was better than anything any of us had walked in with.

## The decisions that shaped the final

The core insight was simple once you said it out loud: registration and transfer are two different intentions. They shouldn't share a mode. They shouldn't even look the same.

The new component starts with a visible toggle—**Register** or **Transfer**—above the search bar. No dropdown, no hidden state. Switch to Transfer and the whole bar transforms: different placeholder, different CTA, different secondary action.

<figure class="post-figure">
  <img src="/assets/projects/spaceship/images/homepage/sps-search-modes.png" alt="The final search component showing Register and Transfer modes with the toggle always visible" />
  <figcaption class="post-figure__caption">Register and Transfer as distinct modes. Different placeholder, different CTA, different secondary action.</figcaption>
</figure>

I talked about using the toggle early on, but in the first prototype it was still hidden—tucked inside the component, visible only after clicking into the search bar. Cleaner. More elegant. We prototyped it that way and brought it to the second round of testing.

## What testing revealed

The second round of usability testing ran on the prototype. The signal was different this time—the big confusion was gone, but new details surfaced. Removal of the subtitle for a cleaner hierarchy. Darkening of the background to give the main component more prominence.

Worth naming: Figma prototypes aren't great for real testing. You can't type, you can't truly feel the interaction. They're far from the browser experience. We worked around it, but it's a constraint that matters.

Then we caught ourselves. We'd spent the entire project removing hidden modes—and here we were introducing a subtler version of the exact same problem. The toggle moved outside. Always visible. No interaction required to discover it.

*Sometimes the most honest design decision trades a bit of elegance for a lot of clarity. The whole project was about making hidden things visible—we couldn't end it by hiding one more.*

## Craft—the details that earn trust

The final component needed to feel intentional at every state. Not just the happy path—the edge cases, the transitions, the moments between actions.

## Outcome

The design shipped April 2024. Weeks later, the BI team flagged an anomaly: widget interactions dropped roughly 95%. It looked like a disaster.

It was the design working as intended.

## What it taught me

**Research before and after, not just before.** Two rounds of usability testing—one on the live product, one on the prototype—kept the team honest. The first round surfaced problems. The second confirmed we'd actually solved them. Most teams skip the second round. That's where the confidence comes from.

**Competing proposals beat consensus.** Three designers working independently produced a richer conversation than any brainstorm. The final design wasn't a compromise—it was a synthesis none of us would have reached alone. The critique format mattered: async first, then live. No pitch decks. No defending. Just the work.

**Clarity over elegance, every time.** The toggle could have been sleeker tucked inside the component. We chose visible over clever—because the entire project was about making hidden things visible. When you catch yourself reintroducing the problem you set out to solve, that's design maturity. Recognising it. Correcting it. Shipping the honest version.

**Fewer interactions can mean better outcomes.** A 95% drop in widget interactions looked like a disaster until you paired it with the transfer success rate. Removing accidental clicks revealed the real signal. The people who meant to transfer were succeeding four times more often. Sometimes the best thing a redesign can do is make the wrong path harder to stumble into.

**Lead the process and the pixels.** Being both the process lead and a designer gave me a different kind of authority in conversations. Not because of the title—because I was in the file. When you're making the work, you earn the right to synthesize the room.

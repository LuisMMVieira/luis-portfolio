Spaceship sells domains. The homepage search bar is the start of the business—register one, or transfer one (or several) in. Two different intentions, one input. Transfer success hovered around 5%. Most of them accidental.

I led the redesign end to end. Four disciplines, 8 people, 2.5 months.

<ArticleSection label="Problem">
<ArticleSubsection>

## The front door was broken

The bar had one job: help you register a domain or transfer one (or more) in. It treated both as variants of the same action, switched by a dropdown labelled "I want to…" that most people missed.

When the bar was focused, a dropdown appeared: "Search or Transfer" and "Bulk transfer." They looked like links, not mode selectors, leaving users confused on what to do. Also, what happened when you hit enter? Search or transfer? A mess...

<figure class="post-figure">
  <img src="/assets/articles/spaceship-search/images/search-before-new.png" alt="The original Spaceship homepage search widget with the 'I want to…' dropdown" />
  <figcaption class="post-figure__caption">The before search widget.</figcaption>
</figure>

The problem wasn't cosmetic. Registration and transfer are completely different intentions. Registration is speculative: "Is this available?" Transfer is intentional: "I own this, move it here." Cramming both behind the same input meant neither worked properly. The dropdown was doing double duty as both a mode switcher and an explanation of what those modes meant—and failing at both.

</ArticleSubsection>
</ArticleSection>

<ArticleSection label="Process">
<ArticleSubsection>

## Four teams, one roadmap

Four teams in the room from day one—Product Platform, Design System, Brand, and Research. One shared roadmap. Research bookending the design work, not bolted on at the end to validate decisions already made.

This was my first time leading process at this scale. I built the roadmap and got it approved before any design work started. Everyone was on board as it made sense from the start.

With some minor tweaks along the way, it became the anchor for the whole project. Every conversation over the two months that followed pointed back to it—six weeks on paper, a bit longer in practice with the holidays sitting in the middle.

<ArticleSection label="Research">
<ArticleSubsection>

## What research actually said

First round of usability testing ran on the live product, in the browser. No prototype comfort blanket. The signal was clean—six issues ranked by severity, the number one problem affecting every single participant.

<figure class="post-figure article-finding article-card">
  <div class="article-finding__nameplate">Research finding #1</div>
  <p class="article-finding__text">"Search or transfer a domain" placeholder creates confusion—users don't understand the difference between search and transfer, especially with the dropdown open.</p>
</figure>

Every participant. Not most—every one. That's not a usability issue, that's a broken product.

<figure class="post-figure article-finding--text-width">
  <img src="/assets/articles/spaceship-search/images/research-findings.png" alt="Severity table of six issues from the first usability round" />
  <figcaption class="post-figure__caption">Six issues surfaced from the first usability round. The top one was universal.</figcaption>
</figure>

The research told us what we suspected but needed proof for: this wasn't a UI polish problem. The entire information architecture of the search component was wrong. Two distinct user intentions were being treated as variants of the same action.

</ArticleSubsection>

## Three directions, one conversation

Prototypes were done, now just needed to prepare the Design Critique session. The idea was straight forward: prepare a Figma file in advance with the 3 directions, invite 4 designers, and give them the prototypes one day in advance to give them time to sit with them and take notes. Worth flagging—the prototypes used handshake domains as examples. A small way to show the bar could scale to other modes.

<ArticleSection label="Decisions">
<ArticleSubsection>

## The decisions that shaped the final

Research confirmed it. Registration and Transfer are 2 different intentions. They shouldn't share a mode.

<figure class="post-figure">
  <div class="post-figure__placeholder" role="img" aria-label="The Figma prototype tested — David's direction with toggle inside the bar"></div>
  <figcaption class="post-figure__caption">The Figma prototype we tested—David's direction, toggle inside the bar.</figcaption>
</figure>

Still, the session caught real things. The search/transfer confusion from Round 1 didn't resurface once. But 3 of 4 participants looked for transfer in the navigation menu—not the search bar. One put it plainly: "It doesn't seem normal to use a search input for a transfer." The component worked. It just didn't announce itself.

From the sessions I also picked up one interesting thing: participants weren't focusing on the component as naturally as they should.

</ArticleSubsection>
</ArticleSection>

<ArticleSection label="Craft">
<ArticleSubsection>

## Craft—the details that earn trust

The final definition gathered all the issues and findings into one component that needed to feel intentional at every state. Not just the happy path—the edge cases, the transitions, the moments between actions.

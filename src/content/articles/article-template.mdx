Every design decision carries weight. The best digital products aren't born from unlimited possibility — they emerge from **thoughtful constraints** that channel creativity into purposeful outcomes. After years of shaping interfaces, I've come to believe that intention beats inspiration every time.

This isn't about following rigid rules. It's about understanding *why* each element exists, what it communicates, and how it serves the person using it.

## The Case for Constraints

When a project begins with a blank canvas and no boundaries, the result is often noise. Too many typefaces, inconsistent spacing, decorative elements that serve no function. Constraints act as a filter — they force you to justify every choice.

> Good design is as little design as possible. Less, but better — because it concentrates on the essential aspects, and the products are not burdened with non-essentials.

Consider a design system with just two typefaces, a strict spacing scale, and a limited color palette. Within those boundaries, you're free to focus on what actually matters:

- **Hierarchy** — what does the reader see first?
- **Rhythm** — does the content breathe between sections?
- **Clarity** — can someone scan this in five seconds and understand the structure?
- **Consistency** — does this feel like it belongs with everything else?

These aren't aesthetic questions. They're communication questions.

## Systems Over Styles

A well-built design system doesn't just make things look consistent — it makes decisions faster. When you've already defined your type scale, your spacing tokens, your color semantics, you stop debating pixels and start solving problems.

<Figure src="/images/Placeholder.png" alt="Design system components arranged in a grid layout" caption="A design system reduces decisions, not creativity. Each token encodes a reason." />

The shift from "what looks good" to "what's appropriate" is subtle but transformative. Style is a byproduct of good systems thinking. When every spacing value maps to a purpose, when every color token has a semantic name, the visual language becomes self-reinforcing.

### Tokens as Design Decisions

A spacing token like `--spacing-md` (20px) isn't arbitrary. It represents a deliberate choice about how much breathing room content needs between related elements. The token *is* the decision — encoded, reusable, and consistent.

The same applies to typography. When `text-l` at 18px with 140% line-height is your long-form body size, you're not choosing a font size for each page. You're applying a principle: readable prose at a comfortable measure.

## Writing as Interface Design

Articles — like this one — are interfaces too. The column width, the paragraph spacing, the heading hierarchy: these are all design decisions that shape how someone experiences your words.

1. **Narrow measure** keeps eyes from drifting across wide screens
2. **Generous line-height** lets text breathe on the vertical axis
3. **Clear heading scale** creates scannable landmarks in long content
4. **Figures that break wider** create visual rhythm and contrast with the text column

The best reading experiences are invisible. You don't notice the typography — you notice the ideas.

---

Intention doesn't mean perfection. It means every element can answer the question: *why are you here?* If it can't, it probably shouldn't be.
